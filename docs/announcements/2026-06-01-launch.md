## Long-form post (Mirror / Substack — ~900 words)

# Princeps — the DeFi prime broker L1 on Reth

I'm building Princeps. This is the public commit, from day one.

*(Princeps — pronounced PRIN-seps. Latin for "first." The title Augustus adopted when founding the Roman Principate. Now: the prime broker stack for on-chain finance.)*

### The thesis

In TradFi, a prime broker is the financial layer between asset managers and the markets. It holds your assets, lends against them, clears trades, and gives you portfolio margin across everything. Without that layer, every fund would manage collateral, financing, and execution separately across every venue — which is exactly the world DeFi lives in today.

DeFi has lots of pieces of a prime broker:

- Aave / Compound — lending
- Lyra / Derive / Aevo — options
- Ribbon / Cega — structured products
- HL / dYdX — cross-margin perps

But these pieces don't share a risk engine. A strategy that wants to sell a covered call, post the underlying as collateral, borrow against it, and rebalance has to do it across four protocols with four oracles, four margin models, and four settlement paths. The composition is brittle, capital-inefficient, and often impossible to execute atomically.

**Princeps unifies the missing pieces on one Reth-based L1 with a single shared risk engine.**

### Why Reth

Two recent moves show what Reth makes possible:

1. **Hyperliquid** reinvented perps by putting the matching engine directly into the L1 — not as smart contracts, as native execution-layer state.
2. **Tempo** is reinventing payments by putting FX and auto-swap directly into the L1.

The pattern: when a domain's math is too hot for the EVM gas model, you move it into the execution layer as a precompile or custom state. You get 10–100× the efficiency. You remove MEV exposure at the contract boundary. You preserve the EVM surface for wallets and developers.

Princeps applies the same pattern to the three primitives perps don't cover:

- **Lending**: liquidations as deterministic state transitions, not keeper auctions. Portfolio margin as canonical state. The toxic-cascade problem that broke DeFi in 2022 stops happening when liquidations execute as part of block production, not as gas auctions during volatility.
- **Options**: Black-Scholes, IV surfaces, Greeks as precompiles. Auto-exercise at the expiry block. Vol surface as canonical chain state. Portfolio margin (Greeks-based) like Deribit, not per-leg margin like every on-chain options DEX today.
- **Structured products**: rebalances execute as protocol rules, not keeper bots. Continuous NAV. Native fee waterfalls, hurdle rates, high-water marks. Vaults become true mutual-fund-style products with real-time entry / exit.

Because all three share one risk engine, complex strategies compose atomically: sell a covered call, post the underlying as collateral, borrow against it, LP the residual — all in one block, no slippage between legs, no MEV between transactions.

### Why no perps

HL already won perps. Building a Princeps perp would be either a worse HL or a parallel HL — both lose. The right move is to be the complement: lending, options, structured products, institutional rails. The L1 that picks up everything HL doesn't.

**If HL is the on-chain CME, Princeps is the on-chain prime broker.**

### The roadmap

- **v0 — Lending (Q3 2026)**: single-asset-pair devnet (USDC collateral, ETH borrow), deterministic sub-second liquidations, portfolio margin. The "Aave on steroids" demo. **Already 8 weeks ahead of plan as of June 1, 2026.**
- **v1 — Options (Q4 2026 / Q1 2027)**: orderbook + vol surface + Greeks precompiles + auto-exercise + cross-margin with lending.
- **v2 — Structured products (Q2 2027)**: native vault and strategy composition over lending + options, continuous NAV, Tempo settlement integration.
- **v3 — Institutional rails (H2 2027)**: KYC overlay (per-tenant opt-in, not chain-wide), fund-admin primitives, decentralized sequencer.

Each version adds primitives; the risk engine extends to cover them.

### The status

Princeps inherits a working Reth + Malachite kernel from openhl — and as of today, the v0 lending kernel is already live in the repo. **369+ tests across 12 crates, fully passing.**

**From openhl (Stages 1–18a)**: consensus substrate, two-validator devnet with real follower replication, CLOB, custom EVM precompiles, funding state machine, full liquidation safety-net (margin math + insurance fund + multi-account scanner + ADL), signed-observation oracle, share-based vault, clearing engine, node coordinator with per-block integration tick.

**v0 lending kernel (Stages 19–22b, shipped 2026-06-01 — about 8 weeks ahead of the v0 plan)**: pure-compute lending crate (market state, kinked IRM, health factor, index-based interest accrual); bridge integration with simulate-then-commit health gating; per-block lending tick; **six EVM precompiles at `0x0c1f`–`0x0c24` covering the entire lending lifecycle** — deposit collateral, borrow, repay, withdraw collateral, health-check, liquidation. Any Solidity contract on Princeps can already call them.

**What's left for v0**: unified scan combining perp + lending risk (Stage 22a), bad-debt absorption routing to the existing insurance fund (Stage 22c), cross-margin engine — the unified portfolio risk model that connects lending and perp positions (Stage 23, the prime broker thesis), single-asset-pair USDC/ETH devnet + CLI demo + sample liquidator bot (Stage 24), public testnet deploy.

The work has been public since May 2026 as the openhl reference implementation. Princeps is openhl growing up: from "open-source reference implementation of an HL-shape L1" to "production platform for the lending / options / structured products DeFi never had."

### How I got here

I came to Reth from Solana. My background is DeFi vault engineering — funding-rate strategies on HL and Drift, delta-neutral on Jupiter, the kinds of products where you spend more time on liquidation cascades and oracle gaps than on the strategy itself. Every time I built one of those vaults, I hit the same wall: the underlying primitives weren't really there. Lending markets that couldn't liquidate fast enough. Options DEXs with per-leg margin that vaporized capital efficiency. Vaults that needed keeper bots to survive a weekend.

I started building openhl in May 2026 as a "what would an HL look like if I built it in the open" learning exercise. Six months in, the kernel was complete enough that the question changed from *"what does HL look like?"* to *"what would the L1 that DOESN'T duplicate HL look like — what would actually fill the gaps in DeFi I keep hitting?"*

That's Princeps. Same kernel, different roof.

### The commitments

- **No perps.** HL owns that; Princeps complements.
- **No token until real revenue.** Tokenizing before product-market-fit kills brand credibility. Earliest token consideration is post-v1, only if real fee revenue exists and tokenization clearly accelerates the product.
- **No fundraise.** Bootstrap solo. Ship v0, prove the model, then decide on growth.
- **Open-source from day one.** Code is public, ADRs are public, roadmap is public.
- **Honest dates.** The roadmap above is what I actually believe is achievable. If slips happen they'll be public too — but right now v0 is running ahead.

### The architectural commitments

Seven decisions, locked for the platform lifecycle:

1. **Consensus**: Malachite (Informal Systems) BFT — instant finality, no reorgs.
2. **Sequencer**: centralized v0–v1, decentralize at v3.
3. **Oracle**: validator-quorum push in EL — no Chainlink dependency, sub-second updates.
4. **Base unit**: USD-stable (USDC default; USDT and Tempo-stable at v1+).
5. **Settlement**: standalone L1 v0–v1, Tempo settlement at v2.
6. **Identity**: anon-default; KYC as Layer-3 per-tenant overlay at v3.
7. **Token**: none until real revenue.

Full ADR document in the repo (`docs/adr/`).
